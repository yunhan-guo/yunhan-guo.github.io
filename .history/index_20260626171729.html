Working Papers

Prediction Market Accuracy: Crowd Wisdom or Informed Minority?

with Roberto Gómez-Cram, Theis Ingerslev Jensen, and Howard Kung

Prediction markets produce remarkably accurate forecasts, but the source of this accuracy is poorly understood. Two explanations dominate: crowd wisdom and insider trading. Using the universe of Polymarket transactions, we show it is neither. Instead, accuracy comes from a minority of persistently skilled traders, around 3% of accounts. Unlike insiders, whose private edge is localized, these traders exhibit depth and breadth. They react to public news when it arrives, eliminate law-of-one-price violations, and trade against the crowd’s behavioral mistakes. The crowd, in turn, generates most of the volume but little of the information, and its losses fund the minority’s profits.

Financial Prediction Markets: A New Measure of Earnings Expectations

with Roberto Gómez-Cram, Theis Ingerslev Jensen, and Howard Kung

We construct a high-frequency measure of earnings expectations using financial prediction markets. Unlike analyst forecasts, which are updated infrequently and prone to agency conflicts, prediction market prices reflect real-time, stake-backed beliefs. Each contract pays one dollar if realized earnings exceed the analyst consensus; we derive the conditions under which these prices represent subjective probabilities. Relative to analyst forecasts, we find that market-implied expectations are (i) more accurate; (ii) free of standard forecast biases; (iii) incrementally informative for earnings announcement returns; and (iv) lead in price discovery. We trace this informativeness to two forces: the relative skill of earnings-market traders, as evidenced by their profitability in non-earnings markets, and insider trading, for which we find suggestive though not conclusive evidence. We expect this measure to become increasingly informative as the market matures, liquidity deepens, and financial prediction markets become part of mainstream finance.

Government Spending and Rising Industry Stars

with Roberto Gómez-Cram, Howard Kung, and Luca Mecca

Government spending is large, persistent, and disproportionately allocated to a select number of firms within industries through federal procurement contracts. To identify the effects on firm outcomes, we construct government spending shocks at the firm level as the difference between realized and expected contract awards. We measure expectations by tracing the allocation of each dollar to firms back to appropriations bills and exploiting predictable agency-to-firm procurement shares. Firms receiving positive government spending shocks increase markups and profitability, outperform industry peers in the stock market, and gain market share, fostering rising industry stars.
